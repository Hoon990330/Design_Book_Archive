New Alphabet

Wim Crouwel – New Alphabet (1967) is a radical typeface designed for the digital age, embracing a grid-based, modular structure that prioritizes machine readability over traditional legibility. Influenced by the limitations of early computer displays, Crouwel’s design features strictly geometric letterforms, challenging conventional typography with its futuristic, experimental approach. Though never widely adopted for practical use, New Alphabet became an iconic example of modernist design, sparking discussions on the relationship between technology and typography. Its influence continues to resonate, shaping contemporary digital and pixel-based type design.

Artist: Wim Crouwel

Year of Production: 1967
